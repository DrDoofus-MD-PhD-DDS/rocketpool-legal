# **Internal Memorandum: Exploring a Legal Structure for pDAO Committees**

**Status:** Internal Reference Document
**Audience:** pDAO Committee Members, Contributors, Core Team Liaisons
**Purpose:** Establish a shared, up-to-date understanding of why and how the pDAO might pursue limited legal structuring—specifically at the committee level—and what that would entail.

---

## 1. Where We Left Off Previously

In earlier rounds of discussion, the pDAO explored legal structuring under significant uncertainty:

* It was unclear whether **U.S.-based legal entities** were viable given regulatory hostility toward crypto, DAOs, and governance tokens.
* There was no clear agreement on whether we were attempting to:

  * Wrap the **entire pDAO**, or
  * Provide limited protection only to **committees and contributors**.
* Offshore options (e.g., Cayman foundations, Swiss associations) were discussed, but these:

  * Carried high costs and operational overhead
  * Required bureaucratic permanence that did not align with Rocket Pool’s culture
* Committee-by-committee entities were considered but raised concerns about:

  * Fragmentation
  * Leadership fatigue
  * Long-term maintenance

At that point, the safest conclusion was often to **do nothing**, largely because the cost, complexity, and uncertainty outweighed the perceived benefit.

---

## 2. Why Consider Legal Structuring at All?

This effort is not about “corporatizing” the DAO or creating leadership hierarchies. It is about **risk management** and **sustainability**.

### 2.1 Personal Protection Without Forcing LLCs on Individuals

Today, committee members:

* Make decisions
* Approve spending
* Interact with third parties
* Sometimes receive stipends

All of this is done **without a legal wrapper**, which can expose individuals—especially visible contributors—to personal liability under theories like general partnership.

The goal is **not** to require:

* Personal LLCs
* Doxxing
* Signing contracts just to volunteer

Instead, the goal is to allow:

> *Protection to follow the committee role, not the individual.*

---

### 2.2 Committees as a Liability Sink

A committee-level legal entity can act as a **liability sink**:

* If something goes wrong, claims are directed at the entity
* The pDAO, Core Team, and individual contributors are less exposed
* The entity can:

  * Hold insurance
  * Indemnify committee members
  * Enter contracts cleanly

This benefits:

* Committee members
* The broader pDAO
* The Core Team, by reducing spillover risk

---

### 2.3 Operational Enablement (Without Centralization)

Currently, the pDAO:

* Cannot sign contracts
* Cannot easily hire vendors
* Cannot accept funds or incentives from third parties
* Avoids business-like activity out of legal caution

A limited committee-focused entity could enable:

* Hiring marketing or design help
* Managing administrative tasks
* Coordinating security responses
* Acting as a neutral executor of pDAO decisions

All **without** claiming to “run” the protocol.

---

## 3. Why We Might Not Do This

It’s important to acknowledge the counterarguments honestly.

### 3.1 Capacity and Energy

* This should not be a solo push
* If there is no sustained interest or ownership, it should not proceed
* Legal structures require *some* care and attention, even minimal ones

### 3.2 Cost Sensitivity

* The pDAO is not well-funded
* Any structure must be cheap, boring, and deferrable
* If the solution costs more than the risk it mitigates, it fails

### 3.3 Practical Reality

* We may never be sued
* We have little money to take
* Many committees already self-limit activity because of resource constraints

In short: **the risk may feel theoretical**.

These objections are valid and should temper any decision.

---

## 4. What Has Changed Since Last Time

Several material changes shift the calculus.

### 4.1 Regulatory Climate (U.S.)

* RPL and rETH are **far less likely** to be treated as securities under current interpretations
* Courts and regulators are increasingly distinguishing:

  * Tokens *themselves*
  * From how they are *used*
* DAOs are now more widely acknowledged as a governance model, even if imperfectly understood

This makes **U.S.-based solutions viable again**, and in many ways preferable:

* Most lawsuits would originate in the U.S.
* Rocket Pool clearly does business in the U.S.
* U.S. courts will assert jurisdiction regardless of offshore structures

---

### 4.2 Tax Environment

* U.S. nonprofit and association law is comparatively flexible
* Committee-level execution does not necessarily create taxable entities
* The pDAO treasury itself does not yet present a pressing tax problem, as:

  * It is not a legal entity
  * Funds are allocated, not distributed as profits
  * Taxation occurs downstream when payments are made

This supports **deferring full pDAO wrapping**.

---

## 5. A New Framing: Committees First, pDAO Later (If Ever)

Rather than attempting to wrap the entire DAO, we focus on:

* GMC (Grants Management Committee)
* IMC (Incentives Management Committee)
* RPIP Editors
* Security Council
* Administrative / coordination functions
* Treasurer role

These are the actors most exposed to:

* Decision-making risk
* Operational risk
* Third-party interaction

The pDAO remains:

* A signaling and approval layer
* Not a legal entity
* Not directly engaged in business activity

---

## 6. The Proposed Structure (Conceptual)

At a high level:

* **pDAO**

  * Approves, overturns, mandates
  * No legal entity

* **Committees**

  * Elected by pDAO governance
  * Execute defined functions

* **Committee Service Entity (DUNA)**

  * Exists solely to support committees
  * Executes pDAO-authorized actions
  * Does *not* control governance or tokenomics

This structure is intentionally **non-hierarchical** and **non-centralizing**.

---

## 7. Why a DUNA Fits This Model Well

A **Decentralized Unincorporated Nonprofit Association (DUNA)** is not a charity by default. It is simply a nonprofit legal form.

### 7.1 Key Advantages

* No equity or ownership
* No profit distribution
* No required member registry
* Supports anonymous or pseudonymous members
* Protection is **role-based**, not identity-based
* Extremely low cost and overhead

Committee members do **not** need to:

* Sign contracts
* File paperwork
* Publicly disclose identities

If you are elected to a committee via pDAO governance, you are covered.

---

## 8. Costs and Practical Overhead

### 8.1 Setup (One-Time)

* Draft charter + legal review: **~$3,000–$5,000**
* Registered agent (optional): **$100–$300 / year**

### 8.2 Ongoing

* No mandatory filings
* No audits
* No BOI reporting
* Optional light legal/accounting review

**Lean annual cost:** $100–$500
**Conservative annual cost:** $1,500–$3,000

This is orders of magnitude cheaper than most alternatives.

---

## 9. Membership, Anonymity, and Elections

Membership can be defined as:

> “Anyone duly elected or appointed to a pDAO committee through approved governance processes.”

This aligns with:

* RPIPs
* Internal votes
* Existing documentation

No legal enrollment is required per member.

---

## 10. What the DUNA Can and Cannot Do

### 10.1 What It Can Do

* Administer committee budgets
* Pay stipends and vendors
* Convert RPL to stable assets for risk management
* Coordinate grants and incentives
* Hold insurance
* Indemnify committee members
* Execute contracts

### 10.2 What It Must Not Do

* Originate or recommend tokenomics changes
* Advocate increasing RPL-based yields (e.g., voter_share)
* Implement burns or value-enhancing mechanisms
* Receive ongoing protocol fees
* Engage in profit-seeking activity

This avoids violating nonprofit principles and avoids conflicts of interest.

---

## 11. RPL Handling and Tax Implications

### 11.1 Committee Funds

* Holding RPL in a Safe: **not taxable**
* Receiving RPL from pDAO allocations: **not income**
* Tax events occur when:

  * RPL is sold or swapped
  * RPL is paid to individuals or vendors

### 11.2 DUNA and RPL

* Holding RPL for spending is acceptable
* Converting RPL → USDC creates accounting obligations but is manageable
* Active trading or revenue generation is discouraged

### 11.3 Stipends

* Stipends are taxable income to recipients
* This is true today, with or without a DUNA
* Paid in RPL → taxed at fair market value at payment time

---

## 12. Why We Are Not Wrapping the Entire pDAO (Yet)

Wrapping the full pDAO would:

* Force identity questions
* Increase tax complexity
* Create representational ambiguity
* Require stronger governance guarantees

At present:

* The pDAO treasury does not demand immediate legal structuring
* Risk concentrates at the committee level

A committee-first approach is incremental, reversible, and aligned with current needs.

---

## 13. Summary

A narrowly scoped DUNA offers:

* Meaningful personal protection
* Minimal cost
* Compatibility with pseudonymity
* A liability sink for committee actions
* Operational enablement without centralization

It does **not** solve every problem, nor does it commit the pDAO to a permanent structure. It simply gives us a **tool** that matches where Rocket Pool is today.
