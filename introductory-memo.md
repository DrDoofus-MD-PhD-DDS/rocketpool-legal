# Draft: A Committee-First Legal and Administrative Structure for the Rocket Pool pDAO

## 1. Purpose and Scope

This document outlines a proposed **committee-first legal and administrative structure** for the Rocket Pool protocol DAO (pDAO). The intent is to reduce legal and personal risk for individuals performing operational work on behalf of the protocol, while preserving decentralization, minimizing overhead, and avoiding premature or unnecessary incorporation of the pDAO itself.

This proposal does **not** seek to wrap or formalize the pDAO as a legal entity at this time. Instead, it focuses on protecting those areas where legal risk is most concentrated: committees, designated roles, and real-world interactions.

---

## 2. Why the pDAO Itself Is Out of Scope (for Now)

### 2.1 Governance vs. Conduct

The pDAO primarily functions as a **governance and signaling mechanism**:

* It votes on protocol parameters
* It allocates resources
* It can overturn committee decisions

The pDAO does **not**:

* Sign contracts
* Hire vendors
* Interact directly with third parties
* Act as a counterparty in the real world

Legal and regulatory risk generally attaches to **active conduct**, not to decentralized signaling or voting. For this reason, wrapping the pDAO itself provides limited immediate benefit relative to its cost and complexity.

---

### 2.2 Treasury and Tax Considerations

The pDAO treasury currently receives RPL via protocol-level token inflation, and those funds are later allocated to committees by on-chain vote.

At present:

* The treasury is not a legal person
* It does not engage in business activity
* It does not contract, market, or transact with third parties
* It functions as a **protocol-level allocation mechanism**, not an operating entity

In practice, tax authorities focus on:

* Identifiable persons
* Executing entities
* Payees and contractors
* Fiat conversion and real-world activity

The **primary tax and liability exposure arises at the point where funds are spent or actions are taken**, not at the point of passive protocol allocation. For this reason, addressing committees and execution layers first materially reduces risk, even while the treasury remains unwrapped.

This approach is consistent with how many decentralized protocols currently operate and does not foreclose future treasury-level structuring if needed.

---

## 3. Design Principles

Any structure adopted should:

* Protect individuals performing DAO-mandated work
* Preserve ultimate authority in the pDAO
* Avoid creating a centralized leadership body
* Minimize legal, administrative, and financial overhead
* Be reversible and adaptable as regulation evolves
* Scale with limited volunteer availability

The goal is **capacity and protection**, not control.

---

## 4. Bodies and Roles Supported

### 4.1 Committees (Decision-Making, Not Execution)

These bodies determine *what* should be done, but do not themselves contract or act as legal counterparties.

#### Grants Management Committee (GMC)

* Determines grant recipients and budgets
* Operates under pDAO-approved mandates
* Receives RPL allocations for grants and administration
* Decisions remain subject to pDAO oversight and reversal

#### Incentives Management Committee (IMC)

* Designs and administers incentive programs
* Coordinates with external protocols conceptually
* No independent authority to bind the DAO or sign agreements

#### Security Council

* Small group of trusted individuals (including core team members)
* Authorized to take **time-sensitive emergency actions** (e.g., parameter changes)
* Authority is:

  * Narrowly scoped
  * Technically defined
  * Time-limited
  * Subject to post-hoc pDAO review

The Security Council is a **failsafe mechanism**, not a governing body.

#### RPIP Editors

* Maintain governance process integrity
* Facilitate proposal creation and lifecycle
* No treasury control or execution authority

#### Treasurer

* Executes approved transfers
* Acts ministerially and procedurally
* No independent spending discretion

---

## 5. Administrative / Coordination Function (New)

As the protocol matures, there is increasing need for coordination that does not cleanly fall within existing committees.

### Purpose

* Prepare and coordinate proposals (e.g., marketing initiatives, roadmaps)
* Source and recommend vendors or service providers
* Coordinate cross-committee efforts
* Serve as an operational interface to the execution entity

### Constraints

* No unilateral authority
* No treasury control
* No ability to override committees or the pDAO
* Acts only pursuant to approved mandates

This function exists to **enable execution**, not to direct strategy.

---

## 6. The Legal Entity (“Service Layer”)

### 6.1 Role of the Entity

A single, limited-purpose legal entity would be created to:

* Sign contracts
* Hire vendors and service providers
* Accept third-party funds or incentives
* Pay contractors and agencies
* Hold insurance
* Provide liability shielding

The entity acts **only on instruction** from the pDAO or its authorized committees and has no independent governance authority.

---

## 7. US-Based Entity Options

With RPL and rETH no longer treated as securities in the U.S., domestic options are viable again.

### Option A: Manager-Managed US LLC (Preferred Baseline)

**Pros**

* Low cost and familiar
* Highly flexible operating agreement
* Easy to insure
* Clear limitation of authority

**Cons**

* Requires designated managers
* Requires careful drafting to avoid “management” optics

**Mitigation**

* Managers act solely as executors of DAO-approved decisions
* Committees are not managers
* Operating agreement explicitly disclaims protocol control

---

### Option B: DAO LLC (Wyoming / Tennessee)

**Pros**

* DAO-aware statutes
* Familiar to crypto participants

**Cons**

* Regulatory uncertainty
* Evolving reporting obligations
* May still require identifiable organizers

---

### Option C: DUNA-Style Structure

**Pros**

* Strong philosophical alignment
* Emphasizes agency rather than ownership
* Natural fit for committee-based systems

**Cons**

* Still legally novel
* Less standardized
* Insurance availability varies

## 8. Automatic Protection Without “Joining” the Entity

A key requirement is that individuals elected or appointed to committees **should not be required to sign legal documents or formally “join” the entity** to receive protection.

This is achievable.

### How Protection Is Provided

Rather than making committee members legal members of the entity, the entity’s governing documents designate them as **“covered persons”** or **authorized agents**, entitled to:

* Indemnification
* Defense
* Insurance coverage

Coverage attaches **by role**, not by membership or signature.

This model is common in:

* Nonprofits
* Foundations
* Contractor-heavy organizations

As a result:

* Protection is automatic upon election or appointment
* Volunteers are not burdened with onboarding paperwork
* Turnover is simple and low-friction

---

## 9. Security Council Coverage

The Security Council should be explicitly included as a covered body, with:

* Clear indemnification
* Insurance coverage
* Narrowly defined emergency authority
* Explicit disclaimers that it is not a management or leadership body

This is particularly important given the time-sensitive and high-impact nature of its actions.

---

## 10. Why a Single Shared Structure Is Preferred

A single shared structure:

* Reduces overhead
* Centralizes insurance and compliance
* Avoids leader scarcity across multiple entities
* Improves continuity across committee cycles
* Keeps execution separate from governance

Committee-by-committee entities would multiply complexity without proportional benefit.

---

## 11. Summary

This approach:

* Protects individuals where risk actually exists
* Avoids premature wrapping of the pDAO
* Preserves decentralization
* Enables real-world interaction
* Keeps costs and bureaucracy low
* Remains reversible and adaptable

In short, it **creates capacity without creating control**.
