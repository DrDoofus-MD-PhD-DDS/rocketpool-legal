# Executive Summary

Rocket Pool's pDAO has work that needs to be done, but no clean legal service layer for doing it.

The current practical problem is not that the pDAO needs a central board or full legal wrapper. The problem is narrower: people doing approved committee and administrative work may need contracts, insurance, indemnification, vendor-payment rails, and liability protection.

The preferred direction is a **single shared service layer** for pDAO committees and administrative functions. A DUNA, or possibly another U.S.-based low-overhead entity, may be the best candidate, but that requires counsel review.

## Proposed approach

The pDAO stays unwrapped for now.

The service layer exists only to support approved work by current and future pDAO-recognized roles, such as:

- GMC;
- IMC;
- RPIP Editors;
- pDAO Treasurer;
- GMC Admin;
- Security Council;
- future administrative or coordination functions approved by governance.

The service layer should be able to sign contracts, hold insurance, pay vendors, pay reasonable stipends, receive approved budgets, administer restricted funds, and indemnify covered participants for good-faith work within scope.

The service layer should not govern Rocket Pool, manage the pDAO, represent all RPL holders, control tokenomics, or act to increase RPL value.

## Why a DUNA may fit

A DUNA is attractive because it is designed for decentralized nonprofit associations. It may allow a group to have legal existence without equity ownership, profit distributions, or a traditional corporate board structure. It may also fit pseudonymous communities better than ordinary LLC structures.

Important caveat: the exact protection, tax treatment, filing burden, membership model, and anonymity model must be reviewed by counsel.

## Main guardrails

The service layer should:

- be narrow and boring;
- serve committees, not lead the DAO;
- avoid tokenomics advocacy in its official capacity;
- avoid profit distributions;
- document conflicts of interest;
- use RPL and stable assets only for budgets and operations, not speculation;
- treat third-party funds as a special risk category;
- preserve the pDAO's ability to approve, reject, or overturn committee action.

## Bottom line

A committee-first service layer could give Rocket Pool the legal and operational tools it is missing without centralizing governance.

The next step is not a final governance vote. The next step is a scoped legal and tax review focused on whether a DUNA or similar structure can provide the desired protection at low cost and low overhead.
