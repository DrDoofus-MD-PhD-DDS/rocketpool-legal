# Third-Party Funds and Incentives

Third-party funds are useful but legally delicate. This document treats them as a special risk category.

## The practical need

The IMC or a future administrative function may want to coordinate incentives with third-party protocols, DeFi venues, exchanges, liquidity pools, or ecosystem partners. Those third parties may offer funds, reimbursements, co-incentives, or other support.

A legal service layer could make this cleaner by providing a party that can receive funds, document terms, and administer payments.

## The risk

If handled badly, third-party funds could make the service layer look like:

- a commercial business-development company;
- a broker or promoter;
- a seller of access to the pDAO;
- a revenue-sharing entity;
- a group monetizing governance influence;
- a manager of protocol economics.

That would undermine the “service layer, not leadership” framing.

## Lower-risk framing

Third-party funds are likely easier to justify when they are:

- restricted to a specific pDAO-approved program;
- used for pass-through incentives or documented expenses;
- not profit distributions;
- not tied to a promised governance outcome;
- not recurring revenue for the DUNA;
- disclosed to the pDAO where appropriate;
- separately accounted for;
- subject to conflict review.

## Higher-risk patterns

The service layer should be cautious about:

- success fees based on RPL/rETH price or volume;
- commissions for integrations;
- recurring business-development revenue;
- undisclosed side payments;
- payments conditioned on pDAO votes;
- payments that require the DUNA to advocate a protocol decision;
- arrangements that look like selling endorsements.

## Suggested policy language

> Third-party funds may be accepted only when they are documented, restricted to an approved program or expense category, not treated as profit of the service entity, not distributed to members except as reasonable compensation for actual work, and not conditioned on the service entity procuring a pDAO governance outcome.

## Accounting questions

For each third-party payment, the service layer should document:

- source of funds;
- purpose;
- restrictions;
- who approved acceptance;
- whether the pDAO was notified;
- whether any committee member has a conflict;
- how funds were spent;
- tax characterization to be reviewed by counsel/accountant.

## Counsel questions

- Can the DUNA receive third-party funds at all?
- Are funds income, contributions, grants, reimbursements, agency funds, or pass-through incentive funding?
- Does receiving funds create unrelated business income or trade/business concerns?
- What documentation is required for non-U.S. protocols or exchanges?
- Could any arrangements trigger broker, money-transmission, sanctions, commodities, securities, or marketing-regulation issues?
- What public disclosure is advisable?
