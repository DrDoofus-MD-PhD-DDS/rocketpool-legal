# Tax and Accounting Questions

This document intentionally frames tax issues as questions for tax counsel or a crypto-competent accountant.

## Important baseline

U.S. tax treatment of digital assets is fact-specific. The IRS has historically treated convertible virtual currency as property for federal tax purposes. Staking rewards and digital-asset payments may create income. Sales or swaps may create gain or loss.

These docs should not state that transfers are “not taxable” without counsel review.

Useful IRS references:

- IRS Notice 2014-21: https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-14-21.pdf
- IRS Revenue Ruling 2023-14 on staking rewards: https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/rr-23-14.pdf

## pDAO treasury receiving RPL inflation

Counsel question:

- Who, if anyone, is the taxpayer when protocol inflation accrues to an unwrapped pDAO treasury?
- Is the pDAO itself treated as a taxpayer, association, partnership, trust, or something else?
- Does the answer change if funds are never distributed for private benefit?

Working note: do not assume “no tax” merely because the pDAO is unwrapped.

## GMC/IMC receiving RPL into a Safe

Counsel question:

- Is a transfer from the pDAO treasury to a committee Safe income to anyone?
- Who controls or beneficially owns the Safe assets?
- Is the Safe acting as agent, nominee, unincorporated association account, partnership account, or something else?
- Does committee discretion over funds change the answer?

## A DUNA holding RPL

Counsel question:

- How is RPL held by the DUNA characterized for tax and accounting purposes?
- Does receipt of RPL create income, contribution, grant funding, or something else?
- What records of cost basis and fair market value are required?

## A DUNA converting RPL to USDC

Working assumption:

- A sale or swap of RPL for USDC may create gain or loss for the entity that owns or is treated as owning the RPL.

Policy recommendation:

- Conversions should be for budgeting and volatility management, not speculation or trading.
- The DUNA should adopt a simple conversion policy and keep records of date, amount, price, wallet, purpose, and approval.

## Committee member stipends paid in RPL

Working assumption:

- Payments to individuals for services are generally compensation income at fair market value when received, whether paid in fiat, USDC, ETH, or RPL.

Counsel/accountant questions:

- Are recipients employees, contractors, grant recipients, or something else?
- What Forms 1099 or other information reporting apply?
- How should payments to non-U.S. persons be handled?
- Can pseudonymous payments be made while maintaining compliance?

## Third-party protocol payments to IMC/DUNA

Counsel question:

- Are third-party payments income, grants, reimbursements, restricted funds, agency funds, or pass-through incentive funds?
- Do recurring payments create trade/business income?
- Could payments create unrelated business income if the DUNA seeks any tax-exempt status?
- Are there sanctions, money transmission, broker, or marketing concerns?

## BOI / CTA reporting

Current public reporting indicates FinCEN issued a 2025 rule narrowing Corporate Transparency Act reporting so domestic companies are exempt from beneficial ownership reporting while foreign reporting companies remain covered.

Useful reference:

- Reuters, “US Treasury's financial crimes unit won't require ownership reporting from US firms” (Mar. 26, 2025): https://www.reuters.com/business/us-treasury-lifts-reporting-requirement-us-firms-anti-money-laundering-law-2025-03-26/

Counsel question:

- Does the current BOI/CTA rule apply to a DUNA or similar domestic entity at formation and during operation?
- Could the rule change before or after formation?
- Are there state-level ownership, organizer, or registered-agent disclosure requirements even if federal BOI is not required?

## Practical accounting recommendation

Even if the structure is low-overhead, it should keep clean records:

- wallet addresses;
- dates and fair market values;
- approvals;
- payees;
- purpose of each transfer;
- invoices or stipend records;
- third-party funding restrictions;
- conversion records;
- year-end summaries.
