# Entity Options Comparison

This document compares possible legal structures at a high level. It is a planning document, not legal advice.

## Design criteria

Rocket Pool likely needs a structure that is:

- low cost;
- low overhead;
- compatible with pseudonymity where possible;
- able to protect committee members and administrative contributors;
- able to sign contracts and hold insurance;
- able to pay contributors and vendors;
- not a wrapper for the full pDAO;
- not a central management body;
- compatible with nonprofit / non-profit-distribution constraints.

## Option 1: Do nothing

Advantages:

- no cost;
- no new bureaucracy;
- no new entity-level tax or filing questions;
- preserves current culture.

Disadvantages:

- committees still lack a legal service layer;
- individuals may keep acting personally or informally;
- no clean insurance or indemnification path;
- difficult to sign contracts, accept funds, or pay vendors;
- does not respond to DAO liability theories.

Best use: acceptable only if the pDAO intentionally avoids business-like activity and accepts the current risk/operational limits.

## Option 2: Personal LLCs for each contributor

Advantages:

- familiar structure;
- may help individual contributors separate personal and project activity;
- can be useful for paid contributors with substantial exposure.

Disadvantages:

- expensive and annoying for volunteers;
- may require doxxing;
- does not create one contract/insurance layer for committees;
- does not solve pDAO-level or committee-level coordination problems;
- unfairly shifts burden to individuals.

Best use: optional personal risk-management layer for highly exposed individuals, not the main pDAO solution.

## Option 3: Separate entity for each committee

Advantages:

- clean separation between GMC, IMC, Security Council, etc.;
- easier to isolate risks by function;
- each entity can have tailored rules.

Disadvantages:

- too much overhead for Rocket Pool's likely capacity;
- repeated registered agents, filings, accounting, policies, and leadership roles;
- harder to maintain when volunteer energy is limited;
- likely over-engineered.

Best use: possibly for a future high-risk committee, but not the starting point.

## Option 4: Regular LLC or nonprofit corporation

Advantages:

- familiar to lawyers, banks, insurers, and vendors;
- clear ability to contract and hold accounts;
- well-understood governance and tax pathways.

Disadvantages:

- may imply centralized managers, officers, or members;
- may require identified members/managers/directors;
- may not fit pseudonymous rotating committee roles;
- may look too much like a management company for the pDAO.

Best use: fallback if DUNA is unavailable, too uncertain, or impractical.

## Option 5: DAO LLC

Advantages:

- designed for DAO-like structures in some jurisdictions;
- may provide entity recognition and liability limitation;
- can be familiar in crypto legal discussions.

Disadvantages:

- may be more appropriate for wrapping a whole DAO than a narrow service layer;
- may require governance links that create centralization or token-holder-member questions;
- may not solve the desire for a non-leadership committee-support structure.

Best use: possible comparison option for counsel, but not the current preferred framing.

## Option 6: Swiss Association

Advantages:

- widely discussed in crypto contexts;
- nonprofit association model can be flexible;
- may provide international credibility;
- may fit committee or protocol-support work.

Disadvantages:

- higher cost and operational overhead;
- foreign jurisdiction complexity;
- less convenient for U.S. litigation, U.S. vendors, and U.S.-based contributors;
- may require more formal governance infrastructure than Rocket Pool wants.

Best use: possible future option if U.S. structures fail or if international regulatory needs dominate.

## Option 7: Cayman foundation or similar offshore foundation

Advantages:

- common in larger crypto projects;
- may be useful for treasury, protocol, or ecosystem foundations;
- can provide a formal foundation layer.

Disadvantages:

- likely expensive;
- heavy overhead for current pDAO resources;
- may be too broad if the pDAO does not want to wrap itself;
- may not avoid U.S. jurisdiction for U.S.-based claims or contributors.

Best use: probably not the current fit; maybe relevant only if Rocket Pool later needs a full ecosystem foundation.

## Option 8: DUNA or similar decentralized nonprofit association

Advantages:

- designed for decentralized nonprofit associations;
- no equity ownership or profit distribution;
- potentially compatible with role-based membership and pseudonymity;
- may provide liability protection, contracting capacity, and insurance access;
- better aligned with a service-layer model than a corporate management entity.

Disadvantages:

- newer structure with less case law;
- practical banking/insurance/vendor acceptance must be tested;
- tax, filing, BOI/CTA, membership, and anonymity issues require counsel review;
- must be carefully scoped to avoid looking like pDAO management.

Best use: current preferred candidate for a single shared committee service layer, subject to legal and tax review.

## Working conclusion

A DUNA or DUNA-like structure appears most aligned with the current project goals, but it should be treated as a candidate, not a conclusion. The key test is whether it can provide meaningful protection and operational utility without creating a central controlling entity.
